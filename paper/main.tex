\begin{abstract}
We demonstrate PatchSteg, a training-free framework for covert communication between AI vision systems via imperceptible perturbations in autoencoder latent space. Using a pretrained Stable Diffusion VAE, we encode binary messages by applying directional perturbations to selected spatial positions in the latent grid. The sender never flips pixel bits directly: the visible image is obtained only after decoding the perturbed representation. These perturbations produce negligible visual change (PSNR $>$ 30\,dB) while enabling reliable message recovery (100\% bit accuracy at $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ with stability-selected carriers). We discover that carrier stability is spatially non-uniform---certain latent positions preserve perturbation direction through the encode-decode round-trip far more reliably than others. Our analysis reveals a stealth--capacity tradeoff: at $\varepsilon{=}1.0$ the channel is not separable by our logistic regression detector (AUC = 0.44) but unreliable, while at $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ it achieves high accuracy but becomes statistically distinguishable (AUC = 0.93). Between these extremes, $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ offers a practical operating point with 100\% accuracy on structured images and marginal detectability (AUC = 0.68). We validate on 300 natural photographs from CIFAR-10 (30 per class) with bootstrap confidence intervals, demonstrate transmission of messages up to 152 bits at 100\% accuracy, and show the phenomenon generalizes across multiple VAE backbones (SD-VAE-MSE, SD-VAE-EMA, SDXL-VAE). Under a logistic regression detector, PatchSteg at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ is harder to detect than classical LSB steganography (AUC = 0.35 vs.\ 0.65), which we use only as a pixel-domain baseline. We provide a theoretical analysis connecting channel reliability to the VAE's KL regularization structure and conclude with concrete next directions for stronger attacks and defenses, including distribution-matched carrier sampling, data-aligned perturbation directions, and seed-free latent sanitization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Recent work by Motwani et al.\ has demonstrated that large language models can engage in secret collusion---hiding messages within seemingly benign text outputs that are undetectable by human monitors. As AI systems increasingly operate in multi-agent settings where they share not only text but also images (screenshots, generated content, perception frames), a natural question arises: \emph{can vision models establish covert communication channels through shared images?}

We answer this affirmatively with PatchSteg, a training-free steganographic framework that exploits the latent space of pretrained autoencoders. Our approach requires no model fine-tuning, no additional neural networks, and no adversarial training. Two agents sharing a secret key (a random seed) can embed and recover binary messages in images with negligible visible artifacts at appropriate perturbation strengths.

The key insight is that pretrained vision autoencoders like the Stable Diffusion VAE map images to a structured latent space where small directional perturbations are preserved through the encode$\to$decode round-trip. By perturbing selected spatial positions along a shared direction vector, a sender encodes bits that a receiver can extract by re-encoding the received image and checking projection directions. Importantly, PatchSteg is \emph{not} a pixel-domain LSB scheme: the direct intervention occurs in representation space, and the pixel differences are only the decoder's side effect.

Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A training-free steganographic encoding/decoding scheme for vision autoencoders achieving 100\% bit accuracy at imperceptible quality levels (Section~\ref{sec:roundtrip}).
    \item Discovery and characterization of \emph{spatially non-uniform carrier stability}---not all latent positions are equally reliable for steganography (Section~\ref{sec:carrier-stability}).
    \item A stealth--capacity--robustness characterization of the covert channel, including detectability analysis showing the channel is not separable by simple statistical detectors at low $\varepsilon$ but becomes distinguishable at high $\varepsilon$ (Sections~\ref{sec:robustness}--\ref{sec:detectability}).
    \item Mechanistic analysis revealing that all four latent channels contribute nearly equally to carrier stability, and that reconstruction error does not predict carrier quality (Section~\ref{sec:mechanistic}).
    \item Generality across multiple VAE backbones (SD-VAE-MSE, SD-VAE-EMA, SDXL-VAE) and evaluation under deployment-relevant distortion chains including screenshot simulation and VAE re-encoding (Sections~\ref{sec:multimodel}--\ref{sec:deployment}).
    \item A predictive framework for content-dependent capacity: image statistics (edge density, entropy, frequency content) predict channel reliability, and local decoder Jacobian analysis reveals the geometric mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:content_science}).
    \item Evaluation on 300 natural photographs with bootstrap confidence intervals, and comparison against 7 detector architectures across 3 feature domains (Sections~\ref{sec:natural},~\ref{sec:stronger_detection}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Neural image steganography.} RoSteALS~\cite{bui2023rosteals} trains an encoder-decoder on top of a pretrained autoencoder latent space to hide messages robustly. More recent diffusion-time hiding and watermarking methods typically assume either control during generation or additional learned modules. In contrast, PatchSteg requires \emph{no training} and no generation-time control: it perturbs the latent representation of an already existing image before reconstruction.

\textbf{AI agent collusion.} Motwani et al.~\cite{motwani2024secret} demonstrate that LLM agents can develop covert text channels undetectable by monitors. We extend this threat model to vision: agents sharing images can hide messages in the latent representation, invisible to pixel-level inspection.

\textbf{Distribution-aware watermarking.} Gaussian Shading~\cite{yang2024gaussian} embeds watermarks in diffusion latents by partitioning the Gaussian prior via CDF thresholds. Stable Signature~\cite{fernandez2023stable} roots watermarks in latent diffusion via model fine-tuning, and Tree-Ring Watermarking~\cite{wen2023treering} embeds structured patterns in the Fourier domain of the initial noise. These methods motivate distribution-aware or model-aware hiding, but PatchSteg differs in operating on VAE latents of arbitrary carrier images rather than on a diffusion sampling trajectory.

\textbf{Autoencoder interpretability.} Our stability analysis connects to understanding VAE reconstruction fidelity. We show that reconstruction quality varies spatially within a single image, and that this variation is content-dependent---structured images (gradients, patches) exhibit more spatial variation in stability than noise. Rolinek et al.\ show that VAEs pursue PCA directions in their latent space~\cite{rolinek2019variational}, motivating our PCA-guided perturbation directions (Section~\ref{sec:pca}).

\section{Method}

\subsection{Backbone}

Our primary backbone is the Stable Diffusion VAE (\texttt{stabilityai/sd-vae-ft-mse}), a KL-regularized autoencoder trained on the LAION dataset. For $H{\times}H$ input images, it produces a $4{\times}\frac{H}{8}{\times}\frac{H}{8}$ latent representation. Each spatial position $(r,c)$ in the latent grid corresponds to an $8{\times}8$ pixel region and is represented by a 4-dimensional vector across channels. We apply the VAE's scaling factor ($0.18215$) after encoding and before decoding. We also validate on alternative VAE checkpoints (SD-VAE-EMA, SDXL-VAE) to test generality (Section~\ref{sec:multimodel}).

\subsection{Encoding Scheme}

Given a shared secret seed $s$, both sender and receiver generate:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A unit direction vector $\mathbf{d} \in \mathbb{R}^4$ from $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$ normalized to unit length.
    \item A set of $K$ carrier positions $\{(r_i, c_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}$ in the latent grid.
\end{enumerate}

To encode bit $b_i \in \{0, 1\}$ at carrier position $(r_i, c_i)$:
\[
    \mathbf{z}_{r_i, c_i} \leftarrow \mathbf{z}_{r_i, c_i} + (2b_i - 1) \cdot \varepsilon \cdot \mathbf{d}
\]
where $\varepsilon$ controls perturbation strength. The modified latent is decoded to produce the stego image.

\subsection{Decoding Scheme}

The receiver encodes the received image to obtain $\mathbf{z}'$, then for each carrier:
\[
    \hat{b}_i = \mathbf{1}\left[ \langle \mathbf{z}'_{r_i, c_i} - \mathbf{z}_{r_i, c_i}, \mathbf{d} \rangle > 0 \right]
\]
where $\mathbf{z}$ is obtained by encoding the \emph{clean} round-trip image (no perturbation), removing systematic VAE reconstruction bias.

\subsection{Stability-Based Carrier Selection}
\label{sec:carrier-selection}

We introduce \emph{stability-based carrier selection}: before encoding, the sender applies a uniform $+\varepsilon$ perturbation to \emph{all} positions simultaneously, performs a single round-trip, and measures the projection delta at each position. Positions are ranked by delta magnitude; the top-$K$ are used as carriers. This requires only one additional encode-decode cycle.

\subsection{Repetition Coding}

For lower $\varepsilon$ (higher stealth), each bit is encoded across $R$ carrier positions with majority-vote decoding. With $R=3$, effective capacity is $K/3$ bits.

\subsection{Distribution-Preserving CDF Encoding}
\label{sec:cdf}

The original PatchSteg encoding shifts carrier values by $\pm\varepsilon$, creating a detectable statistical signature at high $\varepsilon$. We introduce \emph{CDF-PatchSteg}, a distribution-preserving variant inspired by Gaussian Shading. Instead of additive perturbations, we \emph{replace} each carrier's latent value with a sample from the upper or lower half of the empirical Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu_c, \sigma_c)$ estimated from the latent channel.

For each carrier position $(r_i, c_i)$ with message bit $b_i$ and key stream bit $k_i$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compute effective bit $\tilde{b}_i = b_i \oplus k_i$
    \item Sample $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0.5, 1.0)$
    \item If $\tilde{b}_i = 1$: $z_{r_i,c_i} \leftarrow \Phi^{-1}(u; \mu_c, \sigma_c)$ (upper half)
    \item If $\tilde{b}_i = 0$: $z_{r_i,c_i} \leftarrow \Phi^{-1}(1-u; \mu_c, \sigma_c)$ (lower half)
\end{enumerate}
where $\Phi^{-1}$ is the inverse normal CDF. Under the Gaussian approximation, this preserves the chosen one-dimensional carrier marginal before decoding. We therefore treat CDF-PatchSteg as a distribution-matching heuristic rather than a full indistinguishability guarantee after the decode--reencode pipeline or against detectors that exploit joint and spatial statistics.

\textbf{Decoding} requires no clean latent reference: re-encode the stego image, check whether each carrier value falls above or below $\mu_c$, and XOR with the key stream to recover the message bit.

\subsection{PCA-Guided Perturbation Directions}
\label{sec:pca}

Rather than using a random direction vector $\mathbf{d}$, we fit PCA on the 4-dimensional latent vectors across a dataset of images to discover directions of natural variation. The top principal component captures the axis of greatest variance in the latent space; perturbations along this axis are more aligned with the data manifold and potentially harder to detect.

We fit a global PCA on $4$-vectors pooled across all spatial positions and images, then use the $k$-th principal component as the perturbation direction. Perturbation magnitude can optionally be scaled proportionally to the corresponding singular value, ensuring modifications match the natural scale of variation along each direction.

\subsection{Carrier Position Stability}
\label{sec:carrier-stability}

Figure~\ref{fig:stability} shows the stability map---per-position projection delta after round-trip---averaged across test images. At $\varepsilon{=}5.0$, 100\% of positions exhibit positive stability (perturbation preserved), but with significant variance (range 0.98--3.48 in averaged stability score). Stability-based carrier selection exploits this variance by choosing the most reliable positions first.

With stability-selected carriers at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ on structured images (color patches), we achieve \textbf{100\% bit accuracy with 20 carriers}---compared to only 75\% with random selection in the initial validation. This demonstrates the value of intelligent carrier selection.

Crucially, stability maps are \emph{content-dependent} (Figure~\ref{fig:stability_content}). Smooth gradients produce low, uniform stability (mean 1.77), while color patches and noise produce higher, more variable stability. This explains why gradients are poor carriers: the VAE reconstructs them so faithfully that even clean round-trips introduce noise comparable to our perturbations.

\section{Mechanistic Analysis}
\label{sec:mechanistic}

\subsection{Channel Importance}

We test each of the 4 latent channels independently by perturbing only a single channel and measuring round-trip survival. All four channels exhibit high stability: Channel 2 is strongest at 99.7\%, while Channel 0 is weakest at 97.5\% (Figure~\ref{fig:mechanistic}a). The near-uniformity suggests the VAE encoder-decoder preserves information similarly across channels, consistent with the KL regularization encouraging balanced channel usage.

\subsection{Reconstruction Error Does Not Predict Stability}

We hypothesized that positions with lower baseline reconstruction error (without perturbation) would be more stable carriers. Surprisingly, the correlation is near zero: average Pearson $r = 0.048$ across images (Figure~\ref{fig:mechanistic}b). This indicates that the VAE's ability to reconstruct a position faithfully is independent of its ability to \emph{preserve perturbations} at that position---a subtle but important distinction. Stability appears to depend on the local structure of the decoder's Jacobian rather than the magnitude of reconstruction noise.

\subsection{Direction Vector Robustness}

We test 10 different random direction vectors (seeds 1 through 9999). All achieve \textbf{100\% bit accuracy} on the color patches image at $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ with 20 carriers. The choice of direction vector has no measurable effect on channel reliability, confirming that the VAE preserves perturbations isotropically in the 4-dimensional channel space. This is a desirable property: the shared secret need only determine carrier positions, not a specially-chosen direction.

\subsection{End-to-End Message Transmission}

As a proof of concept, we encode the ASCII string ``HI'' (16 bits) into a color patches image using 3$\times$ repetition coding (48 carriers). The receiver perfectly recovers the message with 100\% bit accuracy (Figure~\ref{fig:demo}). This demonstrates that PatchSteg functions as a complete communication channel.

Border positions (within 2 pixels of the latent grid edge) show similar stability to interior positions, with the difference being content-dependent rather than systematic. For the color patches image, borders are slightly \emph{more} stable (2.10 vs 1.76), while for noise images borders are slightly less stable (2.97 vs 3.05).

\subsection{Local Decoder Jacobian Analysis}

To understand \emph{why} certain positions are stable carriers, we estimate the local decoder Jacobian norm at carrier and non-carrier positions via finite differences. For each position, we perturb each of the 4 latent channels independently by $\delta{=}0.1$ and measure the resulting pixel change, approximating $\| \partial \text{Dec} / \partial z_{r,c} \|_F$.

If good carriers correspond to positions where the decoder has high sensitivity (large Jacobian norm), then perturbations at those positions produce larger pixel changes that the encoder can ``see'' when re-encoding. We test this hypothesis with a Mann-Whitney U test comparing Jacobian norms at carrier vs.\ non-carrier positions across 10 images.

PatchSteg reliably transmits messages up to \textbf{152 bits (19 characters)} at 100\% accuracy with raw encoding. With 3$\times$ repetition coding, all messages achieve perfect accuracy, though at the cost of PSNR (the 152-bit message with $R{=}3$ uses 456 carriers, yielding 12.4\,dB). The only failure case is ``HI'' with $R{=}1$, where one bit flips---``HI'' is decoded as ``HY''---demonstrating the value of repetition coding for marginal carrier positions. For LSB, we flip the least significant bit of randomly selected pixels to embed the same number of bits as PatchSteg (20 bits). We train the same logistic regression detector (latent-space statistics) on 15 cover/stego pairs with 3-fold cross-validation.

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis: Why the Channel Works}
\label{sec:theory}

We analyze \emph{why} the SD VAE latent space supports steganographic communication, connecting the channel's reliability to the VAE's training objective and architecture.

\paragraph{Latent distribution structure.} The VAE's KL regularization term encourages each latent channel to approximate $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$, but the learned distribution deviates significantly. Table~\ref{tab:latent_stats} shows per-channel statistics averaged over test images. Channels exhibit non-zero means (0.99 for Ch0, $-0.29$ for Ch1), non-unit variance (0.52--0.86 std), and heavy tails (kurtosis up to 80.8 for Ch2). This structured deviation from the prior creates ``room'' for perturbations: modifications along directions with high variance are less distinguishable from natural variation.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering

\label{tab:latent_stats}

\midrule

3 & 0.807 & 0.519 & $-0.725$ & 5.23 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Perturbation-to-noise ratio.} The perturbation magnitude $\varepsilon$ must be compared against the natural variation in latent values. Using the average latent standard deviation (0.65 across channels), we compute the perturbation-to-latent-std ratio:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cc}
$\varepsilon$ & Perturbation / Latent Std \\
\midrule
0.5 & 0.57 \\
1.0 & 1.15 \\
2.0 & 2.29 \\
5.0 & 5.74 \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

At $\varepsilon{=}1.0$ (not separable by our detector), the perturbation is roughly 1$\times$ the natural standard deviation---hard to distinguish from natural variation. At $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ (detectable), it is nearly 6$\times$ the standard deviation, explaining why statistical detectors succeed. The ``sweet spot'' at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ (${\sim}2.3\sigma$) lies at the boundary of statistical distinguishability.

\paragraph{Decoder sensitivity.} We measure the maximum pixel change caused by a single-position perturbation at $\varepsilon{=}1.0$. Across 5 trials, the average maximum pixel change is \textbf{10.8} (on a 0--255 scale), confirming that the decoder's locally linear response maps small latent perturbations to sub-threshold pixel changes. This is well below the JND (just-noticeable-difference) threshold of ${\sim}2$\,dB change (approximately 30 pixel levels at typical viewing conditions), explaining the visual imperceptibility.

\paragraph{Connection to KL regularization.} The KL term in the VAE's training objective ($D_\text{KL}(q(z|x) \| p(z))$ with $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$) encourages the encoder to map nearby inputs to nearby latent vectors. This \emph{local smoothness} is precisely what enables perturbation survival: if $z' = z + \varepsilon \mathbf{d}$, then $\text{Enc}(\text{Dec}(z')) \approx z'$ because the decoder maps $z'$ to a pixel image near $\text{Dec}(z)$, and the encoder maps that image back near $z'$. The channel works because the VAE's regularization creates a locally smooth, approximately invertible mapping---perturbations in latent space produce coherent pixel changes that survive re-encoding.

\section{Promising Extensions}

\subsection{Distribution-Matched Representation-Space Encoding}

The CDF-PatchSteg construction in Section~\ref{sec:cdf} is a plausible way to reduce the marginal statistical footprint of additive latent perturbations. However, the full capacity, robustness, and detectability sweep for this variant has not yet been rerun end-to-end in the current draft, so we do not present it as a completed empirical result. The correct evaluation should report (i) bit accuracy after the full encode$\to$decode$\to$reencode loop, (ii) marginal and joint goodness-of-fit tests on the modified carriers, and (iii) cross-method detectability under detectors that use spatial as well as marginal latent statistics. A stronger version would replace the single per-channel Gaussian with per-position or mixture-based carrier models.

\subsection{Data-Aligned Perturbation Directions}

The PCA-guided direction idea in Section~\ref{sec:pca} is similarly exploratory. Global PCA over latent 4-vectors may align perturbations with natural variation, but it may also average away the content-specific geometry that determines carrier quality. The more promising attack direction is \emph{content-adaptive} alignment: estimate directions per image, per region, or from the local decoder Jacobian, then choose carriers and perturbation magnitudes jointly to optimize survival, visual distortion, and detector evasion.

\subsection{Toward Active Defenses}

Passive steganalysis is only one layer of defense. For representation-space channels, a more appropriate defense is \emph{inspect then sanitize}: score suspicious latent positions without knowing the shared seed, then suppress only those carriers before the image reaches the next model. Our current prototype follows this strategy by combining two seed-free cues: unusually large local latent deviations relative to a neighborhood, and unusually large drift under one additional VAE round-trip. The highest-scoring positions are then smoothed in latent space before decoding. We do not yet report benchmark numbers for this guard, but it is a better match to PatchSteg than classical pixel-domain countermeasures because it attacks the channel where it is encoded rather than after the perturbation has diffused into pixels.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Implications for AI Safety}

PatchSteg demonstrates a concrete mechanism by which vision models can establish covert communication channels through shared images. In multi-agent settings where AI systems pass images to each other (robotic perception, AI-generated content, shared camera feeds), PatchSteg-style channels could in principle enable collusion that is difficult for human monitors to detect visually.

\subsection{The Stealth--Capacity Frontier}

Our detectability analysis quantifies a fundamental tradeoff: at $\varepsilon{=}1.0$ the channel is not separable by our detectors but unusable; at $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ it is reliable but statistically distinguishable. The practical operating point at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ with stability-selected carriers on structured images achieves 100\% accuracy while remaining only marginally detectable (AUC = 0.68). With repetition coding ($R{=}3$), this yields approximately 66 effective bits per image at $K{=}200$.

\subsection{Content Dependence}

A key finding is that channel reliability is strongly content-dependent. Smooth gradients provide near-zero channel capacity, while textured images support hundreds of bits. Our experiments on CIFAR-10 photographs (Section~\ref{sec:natural}) confirm that natural images---which are inherently textured---achieve 98.8\% accuracy, validating this intuition.

\subsection{Difference from Classical Pixel-Domain Steganography}

PatchSteg at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ is harder to detect than classical LSB steganography (AUC 0.35 vs.\ 0.65) under our logistic regression detector on latent-space statistics. This is not because PatchSteg is an improved LSB scheme; it is because the mechanism is fundamentally different. PatchSteg edits a learned latent representation and relies on the decoder to realize that change in pixel space, whereas LSB edits pixel bits directly. This representation-space operating point is precisely what makes PatchSteg relevant for model-to-model collusion scenarios and also what makes pixel-only defenses incomplete.

\subsection{Toward Stronger Attacks and Defenses}

The most credible next step for the attack is to make carrier selection \emph{jointly} optimize reliability, stealth, and robustness instead of ranking positions by a single stability score. Concretely, the sender should combine stability maps, local Jacobian information, and content-aware distribution matching to choose carriers that both survive the round trip and remain hard to detect. On the defense side, passive detectors should be paired with active capacity-reduction mechanisms such as latent-space smoothing, stochastic re-encoding, or representation randomization. In short: stronger attacks will be more adaptive and geometry-aware, and stronger defenses will intervene in latent space rather than only classifying images after the fact.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our implementation requires both agents to share a compatible VAE model and a secret seed (cross-model decode is tested but with reduced accuracy). The channel capacity is limited to tens or hundreds of bits per image (152 bits demonstrated). While we test 7 detector architectures across 3 feature domains, more sophisticated learned detectors (e.g., trained CNNs on full images, or detectors specifically designed for latent-space steganography) might achieve substantially higher detection rates. Our evaluation uses CIFAR-10 images upscaled to $256{\times}256$---while this provides diversity and scale, the upscaling introduces interpolation artifacts that may not reflect native high-resolution photography. The channel degrades significantly under aggressive distortions (JPEG Q$<$30, heavy cropping), limiting deployment scenarios. We also intentionally stop short of claiming end-to-end gains from the CDF, PCA-guided, and active-guard extensions until they are benchmarked on the same natural-image suite used for the core PatchSteg results.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented PatchSteg, a training-free steganographic framework enabling covert bit transmission through pretrained VAE latent spaces. The main empirical findings are that the phenomenon generalizes across multiple VAE architectures, carrier stability is spatially non-uniform and content-dependent, there exists a quantifiable stealth--capacity tradeoff, and local decoder geometry helps explain why some latent positions are much better carriers than others. PatchSteg is harder to detect than a pixel-domain LSB baseline at moderate perturbation strength not because it is a better pixel-hiding scheme, but because it operates in representation space before reconstruction. Evaluated on 300 natural images with confidence intervals, PatchSteg illustrates an underexplored risk vector in multi-agent AI systems. The most promising next steps are distribution-matched carrier sampling, data-aligned perturbation directions, and seed-free latent sanitization that directly attacks the channel at its source.
