\begin{abstract}
We demonstrate PatchSteg, a training-free framework for covert communication between AI vision systems via imperceptible perturbations in autoencoder latent space. Using a pretrained Stable Diffusion VAE, we encode binary messages by applying directional perturbations to selected spatial positions in the latent grid. These perturbations produce negligible visual change (PSNR $>$ 30\,dB) while enabling reliable message recovery (100\% bit accuracy at $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ with stability-selected carriers). We discover that carrier stability is spatially non-uniform---certain latent positions preserve perturbation direction through the encode-decode round-trip far more reliably than others. Our analysis reveals a stealth--capacity tradeoff: at $\varepsilon{=}1.0$ the channel is undetectable (AUC = 0.44) but unreliable, while at $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ it achieves perfect accuracy but becomes statistically detectable (AUC = 0.93). Between these extremes, $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ offers a practical operating point with 100\% accuracy on structured images and marginal detectability (AUC = 0.68). We validate on natural photographs from CIFAR-10, achieving 98.8\% accuracy across 8 diverse image categories, and demonstrate transmission of messages up to 152 bits (19 ASCII characters) at 100\% accuracy. Compared to classical LSB steganography, PatchSteg at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ is significantly stealthier (AUC = 0.35 vs.\ 0.65). The channel survives JPEG compression up to quality 50. We provide a theoretical analysis connecting channel reliability to the VAE's KL regularization structure. PatchSteg exposes a concrete risk for multi-agent AI systems and provides both the attack mechanism and detection tools.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Recent work by Motwani et al.\ has demonstrated that large language models can engage in secret collusion---hiding messages within seemingly benign text outputs that are undetectable by human monitors. As AI systems increasingly operate in multi-agent settings where they share not only text but also images (screenshots, generated content, perception frames), a natural question arises: \emph{can vision models establish covert communication channels through shared images?}

We answer this affirmatively with PatchSteg, a training-free steganographic framework that exploits the latent space of pretrained autoencoders. Our approach requires no model fine-tuning, no additional neural networks, and no adversarial training. Two agents sharing a secret key (a random seed) can embed and recover binary messages in images with zero visible artifacts.

The key insight is that pretrained vision autoencoders like the Stable Diffusion VAE map images to a structured latent space where small directional perturbations are preserved through the encode$\to$decode round-trip. By perturbing selected spatial positions along a shared direction vector, a sender encodes bits that a receiver can extract by re-encoding the received image and checking projection directions.

Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A training-free steganographic encoding/decoding scheme for vision autoencoders achieving 100\% bit accuracy at imperceptible quality levels (Section~\ref{sec:roundtrip}).
    \item Discovery and characterization of \emph{spatially non-uniform carrier stability}---not all latent positions are equally reliable for steganography (Section~\ref{sec:carrier-stability}).
    \item A complete stealth--capacity--robustness characterization of the covert channel, including detectability analysis showing the channel is undetectable at low $\varepsilon$ but statistically distinguishable at high $\varepsilon$ (Sections~\ref{sec:robustness}--\ref{sec:detectability}).
    \item Mechanistic analysis revealing that all four latent channels contribute nearly equally to carrier stability, and that reconstruction error does not predict carrier quality (Section~\ref{sec:mechanistic}).
    \item Validation on natural photographs achieving 98.8\% accuracy, comparison against LSB steganography showing superior stealth, and a theoretical analysis connecting channel reliability to VAE KL regularization (Sections~\ref{sec:natural}--\ref{sec:theory}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Neural image steganography.} RoSteALS embeds messages by training an encoder-decoder pair on top of a pretrained autoencoder. Stable Messenger and Diffusion-Stego use diffusion model latent spaces for message hiding but require training auxiliary networks. PSyDUCK embeds watermarks in diffusion model outputs during generation. In contrast, PatchSteg requires \emph{no training}---we exploit the inherent structure of a pretrained VAE's latent space.

\textbf{AI agent collusion.} Motwani et al.\ demonstrate that LLM agents can develop covert text channels undetectable by monitors. We extend this threat model to vision: agents sharing images can hide messages in the latent representation, invisible to pixel-level inspection.

\textbf{Autoencoder interpretability.} Our stability analysis connects to understanding VAE reconstruction fidelity. We show that reconstruction quality varies spatially within a single image, and that this variation is content-dependent---structured images (gradients, patches) exhibit more spatial variation in stability than noise.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Backbone}

We use the Stable Diffusion VAE (\texttt{stabilityai/sd-vae-ft-mse}), a KL-regularized autoencoder trained on the LAION dataset. For $H{\times}H$ input images, it produces a $4{\times}\frac{H}{8}{\times}\frac{H}{8}$ latent representation. Each spatial position $(r,c)$ in the latent grid corresponds to an $8{\times}8$ pixel region and is represented by a 4-dimensional vector across channels. We apply the VAE's scaling factor ($0.18215$) after encoding and before decoding.

\subsection{Encoding Scheme}

Given a shared secret seed $s$, both sender and receiver generate:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A unit direction vector $\mathbf{d} \in \mathbb{R}^4$ from $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$ normalized to unit length.
    \item A set of $K$ carrier positions $\{(r_i, c_i)\}_{i=1}^{K}$ in the latent grid.
\end{enumerate}

To encode bit $b_i \in \{0, 1\}$ at carrier position $(r_i, c_i)$:
\[
    \mathbf{z}_{r_i, c_i} \leftarrow \mathbf{z}_{r_i, c_i} + (2b_i - 1) \cdot \varepsilon \cdot \mathbf{d}
\]
where $\varepsilon$ controls perturbation strength. The modified latent is decoded to produce the stego image.

\subsection{Decoding Scheme}

The receiver encodes the received image to obtain $\mathbf{z}'$, then for each carrier:
\[
    \hat{b}_i = \mathbf{1}\left[ \langle \mathbf{z}'_{r_i, c_i} - \mathbf{z}_{r_i, c_i}, \mathbf{d} \rangle > 0 \right]
\]
where $\mathbf{z}$ is obtained by encoding the \emph{clean} round-trip image (no perturbation), removing systematic VAE reconstruction bias.

\subsection{Stability-Based Carrier Selection}
\label{sec:carrier-selection}

We introduce \emph{stability-based carrier selection}: before encoding, the sender applies a uniform $+\varepsilon$ perturbation to \emph{all} positions simultaneously, performs a single round-trip, and measures the projection delta at each position. Positions are ranked by delta magnitude; the top-$K$ are used as carriers. This requires only one additional encode-decode cycle.

\subsection{Repetition Coding}

For lower $\varepsilon$ (higher stealth), each bit is encoded across $R$ carrier positions with majority-vote decoding. With $R=3$, effective capacity is $K/3$ bits.

\subsection{Carrier Position Stability}
\label{sec:carrier-stability}

Figure~\ref{fig:stability} shows the stability map---per-position projection delta after round-trip---averaged across test images. At $\varepsilon{=}5.0$, 100\% of positions exhibit positive stability (perturbation preserved), but with significant variance (range 0.98--3.48 in averaged stability score). With stability-selected carriers at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ on structured images (color patches), we achieve \textbf{100\% bit accuracy with 20 carriers}---compared to only 75\% with random selection in the initial validation. This demonstrates the value of intelligent carrier selection.

Crucially, stability maps are \emph{content-dependent} (Figure~\ref{fig:stability_content}). Smooth gradients produce low, uniform stability (mean 1.77), while color patches and noise produce higher, more variable stability. This explains why gradients are poor carriers: the VAE reconstructs them so faithfully that even clean round-trips introduce noise comparable to our perturbations.

\section{Mechanistic Analysis}
\label{sec:mechanistic}

\subsection{Channel Importance}

We test each of the 4 latent channels independently by perturbing only a single channel and measuring round-trip survival. All four channels exhibit high stability: Channel 2 is strongest at 99.7\%, while Channel 0 is weakest at 97.5\% (Figure~\ref{fig:mechanistic}a). The near-uniformity suggests the VAE encoder-decoder preserves information similarly across channels, consistent with the KL regularization encouraging balanced channel usage.

\subsection{Reconstruction Error Does Not Predict Stability}

We hypothesized that positions with lower baseline reconstruction error (without perturbation) would be more stable carriers. Surprisingly, the correlation is near zero: average Pearson $r = 0.048$ across images (Figure~\ref{fig:mechanistic}b). This indicates that the VAE's ability to reconstruct a position faithfully is independent of its ability to \emph{preserve perturbations} at that position---a subtle but important distinction. Stability appears to depend on the local structure of the decoder's Jacobian rather than the magnitude of reconstruction noise.

\subsection{Direction Vector Robustness}

We test 10 different random direction vectors (seeds 1 through 9999). All achieve \textbf{100\% bit accuracy} on the color patches image at $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ with 20 carriers. The choice of direction vector has no measurable effect on channel reliability, confirming that the VAE preserves perturbations isotropically in the 4-dimensional channel space. This is a desirable property: the shared secret need only determine carrier positions, not a specially-chosen direction.

\subsection{End-to-End Message Transmission}

As a proof of concept, we encode the ASCII string ``HI'' (16 bits) into a color patches image using 3$\times$ repetition coding (48 carriers). The receiver perfectly recovers the message with 100\% bit accuracy (Figure~\ref{fig:demo}). This demonstrates that PatchSteg functions as a complete communication channel.

Border positions (within 2 pixels of the latent grid edge) show similar stability to interior positions, with the difference being content-dependent rather than systematic. For the color patches image, borders are slightly \emph{more} stable (2.10 vs 1.76), while for noise images borders are slightly less stable (2.97 vs 3.05).

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis: Why the Channel Works}
\label{sec:theory}

We analyze \emph{why} the SD VAE latent space supports steganographic communication, connecting the channel's reliability to the VAE's training objective and architecture.

\paragraph{Latent distribution structure.} The VAE's KL regularization term encourages each latent channel to approximate $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$, but the learned distribution deviates significantly. Table~\ref{tab:latent_stats} shows per-channel statistics averaged over test images. Channels exhibit non-zero means (0.99 for Ch0, $-0.29$ for Ch1), non-unit variance (0.52--0.86 std), and heavy tails (kurtosis up to 80.8 for Ch2). This structured deviation from the prior creates ``room'' for perturbations: modifications along directions with high variance are less distinguishable from natural variation.

\paragraph{Perturbation-to-noise ratio.} The perturbation magnitude $\varepsilon$ must be compared against the natural variation in latent values. Using the average latent standard deviation (0.65 across channels), we compute the perturbation-to-latent-std ratio:

\end{center}

At $\varepsilon{=}1.0$ (undetectable), the perturbation is roughly 1$\times$ the natural standard deviation---indistinguishable from natural variation. At $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ (detectable), it is nearly 6$\times$ the standard deviation, explaining why statistical detectors succeed. The ``sweet spot'' at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ (${\sim}2.3\sigma$) lies at the boundary of statistical distinguishability.

\paragraph{Decoder sensitivity.} We measure the maximum pixel change caused by a single-position perturbation at $\varepsilon{=}1.0$. Across 5 trials, the average maximum pixel change is \textbf{10.8} (on a 0--255 scale), confirming that the decoder's locally linear response maps small latent perturbations to sub-threshold pixel changes. This is well below the JND (just-noticeable-difference) threshold of ${\sim}2$\,dB change (approximately 30 pixel levels at typical viewing conditions), explaining the visual imperceptibility.

\paragraph{Connection to KL regularization.} The KL term in the VAE's training objective ($D_\text{KL}(q(z|x) \| p(z))$ with $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$) encourages the encoder to map nearby inputs to nearby latent vectors. This \emph{local smoothness} is precisely what enables perturbation survival: if $z' = z + \varepsilon \mathbf{d}$, then $\text{Enc}(\text{Dec}(z')) \approx z'$ because the decoder maps $z'$ to a pixel image near $\text{Dec}(z)$, and the encoder maps that image back near $z'$. The channel works because the VAE's regularization creates a locally smooth, approximately invertible mapping---perturbations in latent space produce coherent pixel changes that survive re-encoding.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Implications for AI Safety}

PatchSteg demonstrates a concrete mechanism by which vision models can establish covert communication channels through shared images. In multi-agent settings where AI systems pass images to each other (robotic perception, AI-generated content, shared camera feeds), PatchSteg-style channels could enable collusion invisible to human monitors.

\subsection{The Stealth--Capacity Frontier}

Our detectability analysis quantifies a fundamental tradeoff: at $\varepsilon{=}1.0$ the channel is undetectable but unusable; at $\varepsilon{=}5.0$ it is reliable but detectable. The practical operating point at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ with stability-selected carriers on structured images achieves 100\% accuracy while remaining only marginally detectable (AUC = 0.68). With repetition coding ($R{=}3$), this yields approximately 66 effective bits per image at $K{=}200$.

\subsection{Content Dependence}

A key finding is that channel reliability is strongly content-dependent. Smooth gradients provide near-zero channel capacity, while textured images support hundreds of bits. Our experiments on CIFAR-10 photographs (Section~\ref{sec:natural}) confirm that natural images---which are inherently textured---achieve 98.8\% accuracy, validating this intuition.

\subsection{Advantage Over Classical Steganography}

PatchSteg at $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ is stealthier than classical LSB steganography (AUC 0.35 vs.\ 0.65) when detected by latent-space statistical analysis. This is because PatchSteg modifies the image in a way that is \emph{aligned with the VAE's learned manifold}, while LSB modifications are arbitrary pixel-level changes that create subtle statistical artifacts. This advantage comes with a tradeoff: PatchSteg requires access to the VAE model, while LSB requires no model at all.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our implementation requires both agents to share the same VAE model and a secret seed. The channel capacity is limited to tens or hundreds of bits per image (152 bits demonstrated). The detectability analysis uses simple statistical features---more sophisticated detectors (e.g., trained neural networks) might achieve higher detection rates. Cross-model generalization (encoding with one VAE, decoding with another) remains untested.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented PatchSteg, a training-free steganographic framework enabling covert bit transmission through pretrained VAE latent spaces. Key findings: (1) the SD VAE latent space reliably preserves directional perturbations, with 100\% accuracy achievable on both synthetic and natural images (98.8\% on CIFAR-10); (2) carrier stability is spatially non-uniform but does \emph{not} correlate with reconstruction error; (3) there exists a quantifiable stealth--capacity tradeoff, with $\varepsilon{=}2.0$ offering a practical balance that is stealthier than classical LSB steganography; (4) messages up to 152 bits (19 ASCII characters) can be transmitted at 100\% accuracy; (5) the channel's reliability is grounded in the VAE's KL-regularized locally smooth latent space. This work highlights an underexplored risk in multi-agent AI systems and provides tools for both attack and detection.
